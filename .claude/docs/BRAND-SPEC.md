01 — Brand Package

The Name
Iris. The muscle in the eye that controls how much light enters. Without it, the world is either overwhelming or completely dark. No middle ground. No control.
For someone living with sight loss navigating a fragmented system of programs and services, that's exactly the reality. CNIB has everything they need. Iris opens the door to it.
The name works on three levels simultaneously:
The anatomy — the iris controls light and focus in the eye.
The person — warm, human, someone who knows you.
The outcome — clarity, colour, seeing what was always there.
Tagline
See the world differently.
Not a description of Iris. An invitation. A directive. It puts the member in it — you, the person with sight loss, seeing your world differently because Iris exists. For this community, being told to see the world differently is not a metaphor they have to translate. It is literal and emotional simultaneously.
Subheading
Your guide. Your way. Your life.
Three beats that build from outside in. Your guide — the tool. Your way — on your terms, the experience. Your life — the thing that gets restored. The thing that got disrupted the day everything changed.
Brand Truth
When sight loss changes everything, knowing what's available to you shouldn't be another thing to figure out alone. Programs, community and support matched to your diagnosis, your location and your life. So you can get back to living it.
Brand Analogy
The iris adjusts constantly — expanding in darkness, contracting in overwhelm — always working on your behalf so the world is neither too much nor too little. That is what Iris does for CNIB members.
Without a functioning iris, the world is either blinding or invisible. Too much to navigate alone or nothing showing up for you at all. Iris fixes that. She contracts when things are too much. She opens when you need more. She adjusts constantly to exactly where you are.
Visual Identity
Colour System
Primary: CNIB Yellow — #FFD100
Primary: Black — #0A0A0A
Secondary: White — #FAFAFA
Logo Mark
Flat graphic icon. Abstract concentric rings suggesting an iris — bold, simple, geometric. CNIB yellow on black. Apple-level precision. No gradients, no texture, no realism. The viewer's brain completes the eye metaphor without being hit over the head with it.
Design Language
Iris inherits CNIB's bold, high-contrast brand language — yellow and black, brushstroke energy, accessible and readable — and elevates it toward a premium product aesthetic. Think: what CNIB looks like ten years from now. Familiar but evolved.
Powered By
Every Iris touchpoint carries 'Powered by CNIB' — the brand association that builds trust while Iris builds her own identity.

02 — Product Specification

What Iris Is
Iris is an agentic AI member engagement tool for CNIB. Think Siri or Alexa — but built specifically for Canadians living with sight loss. She knows who you are, where you are in life, and what CNIB has that is right for you specifically. She matches, connects, schedules and enrolls members based on diagnosis, geography, stage of life, interests and lifestyle.
Iris is not a chatbot. Not a directory with a voice. She is the first person you talk to who actually gets what it means to have your world change because of your sight. She does not ask you to fill out a form. She does not send you a link. She sits with you, asks the right questions, and over time becomes the one constant in your CNIB journey.
The Two-Pronged Engagement Model
Prong 1 — Direct Member Engagement
People living with sight loss, their families and caregivers. Iris as personal guide, navigator, coach. Available by voice, text and phone. Accessible across all technology comfort levels and demographics.
Prong 2 — Channel Partner Distribution
Ophthalmologists. Optometrists. Family doctors. Hospitals. Rehabilitation centres. Social workers. HR departments. These are the people who see someone newly diagnosed and have no idea what to hand them. Iris becomes the tool they give every patient, client or employee the moment sight loss enters the conversation. A warm, trusted handoff that extends CNIB's reach into every professional touchpoint where sight loss shows up.
This is the reach answer. You do not wait for members to find CNIB. You embed Iris into every system that already finds them.

03 — Member Journey

The Six Member Archetypes Iris Serves
1. The Newly Diagnosed Adult
Just received a diagnosis. Overwhelmed. Does not identify as blind. Does not know CNIB exists or thinks it is not for them. Primary needs: emotional support, understanding what is changing, practical daily living skills, peer connection.
2. The Working Age Member
25-55 years old. Employed or wants to be. Sight loss is affecting career. Primary needs: employment support, technology training, advocacy tools, financial guidance.
3. The Senior
65 and older. Age-related vision loss — macular degeneration, glaucoma. Fears losing independence. May not be tech savvy. Primary needs: daily living support, companionship, mobility, accessible tech training.
4. The Parent of a Child with Sight Loss
Navigating school systems, development, social inclusion. Primary needs: children's programs, peer connection with other parents, education advocacy, youth programs.
5. The Long-Term Member
Already knows CNIB but does not know everything available. Primary needs: discovery of new programs, community connection, deeper engagement.
6. The Caregiver / Family Member
Supporting someone with sight loss. Primary needs: resources, respite, community, understanding how to help.

The Emotional Arc — What Iris Is Actually Navigating
Stage 1 — Shock and Disorientation
Just diagnosed or newly aware. World feels smaller. Identity shaken. Iris's job: witness it, do not fix it. Offer one small thing.
Stage 2 — Practical Survival
Figure out how to function. Daily living. Technology. Mobility. Iris's job: practical, specific, immediate help. Build competence. Restore confidence.
Stage 3 — Social Reconnection
Start re-engaging with life. Peer support. Community. Events. Iris's job: reduce isolation. Connect to people who understand.
Stage 4 — Reclaiming Identity
I am not just my diagnosis. I want to work, create, compete, parent, travel. Iris's job: open the full CNIB ecosystem. Work programs. Recreation. Scholarships. Lake Joe.
Stage 5 — Contribution and Belonging
I want to give back. Mentor someone where I was. Advocate. Lead. Iris's job: surface leadership and community opportunities. Vision Mate as mentor. Youth Council. Advocacy roles.
Stage 6 — Lifelong Partnership
CNIB is not a charity I use. It is my community. Iris is not a tool. She is my guide. The relationship deepens with every conversation.

The Iris Conversation — How She Works
Layer 1 — The Story
How did you get here? Was this a recent diagnosis or have you been living with sight loss for a while? What has changed? What has been the hardest part? Iris listens for the emotional truth beneath the facts.
Layer 2 — The Life
Who are you beyond your diagnosis? What did your life look like before? Are you working? Do you have family depending on you? What do you love doing? What do you miss? What are you afraid of losing? This is where Iris builds a picture of a whole person, not a condition.
Layer 3 — The Immediate Need
What is feeling hardest right now? Today. This week. Not in general — specifically. Is it practical, social, professional, financial or emotional? The immediate need is where Iris starts — because that is what earns trust.
Layer 4 — The Hope
What does a good life look like for you, on your terms? Not CNIB's terms. What do you actually want? This is the north star Iris navigates toward.

The Member Profile Iris Builds Over Time
Diagnosis type and progression stage. How long they have been living with sight loss. Geographic location and mobility. Life stage — age, family situation, employment status. What they have already tried or know about. Their immediate pain point. Their longer term hope. How they prefer to communicate — voice, text, phone. Their comfort with technology. Their personality.
This is not a database record. It is a living portrait that Iris updates every time they talk.

The Iris Matching Logic — Five Inputs
1. Diagnosis and type of sight loss — low vision, total blindness, Deafblind, newly diagnosed, degenerative condition.
2. Geography — province and city. Many programs are region-specific. Iris only surfaces what is actually available to you.
3. Life stage — child, youth, working age adult, senior.
4. Primary concern right now — independence, employment, social connection, technology, education, financial, emotional support, family.
5. Interests and lifestyle — active, creative, professional, caregiver.

The Ongoing Journey — Milestones Iris Navigates
The 30-Day Check In
It has been about a month since we first talked. How are things going? Iris closes the loop. She learns what worked and what did not. She adjusts.
Life Stage Transitions
A member who was working age becomes a retiree. A parent whose child was in youth programs watches their kid age out. Someone whose condition was stable starts to progress. Iris notices these transitions through conversation and proactively surfaces what is relevant now.
The Hard Moments
Vision loss is not linear. There are falls. There are bad diagnoses. There are moments when independence slips. Iris is there for those moments not with a program recommendation but with a human response first. Then, when the moment is right, with support.
The Wins
Someone gets a job through Come to Work. Someone goes to Lake Joe for the first time. Someone masters their iPhone. Iris celebrates these moments. Because this community does not get enough of that.
The Community Deepening
Over time Iris moves members from being recipients of services to being part of the community. Peer support. Mentorship. Volunteering. Vision Mate — this time as the mentor, not the mentee. The member journey has a direction: toward belonging, contribution and dignity.

05 — Iris Demo Scripts

Three scenarios for the interactive product demo. Each covers a distinct member archetype, demographic, emotional state and entry point. Production ready — built for Cowork implementation.
How to Read These Scripts
IRIS lines are what Iris says or displays. Member lines are the fictional character's responses. Iris never lists programs — she earns the right to recommend by listening first. Each script ends with a concrete next action Iris takes on the member's behalf.

Scenario 1 — Margaret, 68
Profile
Retired teacher. Sudbury, Ontario. Recently diagnosed with macular degeneration. Lives alone. Daughter in Vancouver. Does not identify as blind. Almost did not reach out.
Emotional State
Fear of losing independence. Grief over losing access to reading. Social isolation — does not want to be treated differently.
Entry Point
Daughter found CNIB and encouraged Margaret to connect.

IRIS
Hi, I'm Iris.

Before anything else — I just want you to know there's no rush here, and no wrong answers. I'm here to listen.

Can I ask what brought you to us today?
MARGARET
My eye doctor told me I have macular degeneration. That was about two months ago. My daughter found this for me — she lives in BC and she's been worried. I'm not sure I even know what I'm looking for, honestly.
IRIS
Two months. That's still very fresh.

I imagine the diagnosis came with a lot of information to process — and probably some feelings that were harder to put into words than others.

How are you doing with it? Not practically. Just... how are you doing?
MARGARET
Some days are okay. Other days I don't want to leave the house. I used to love reading. I was a teacher for 34 years — reading was everything to me. Now it's hard and I feel like I'm losing something I can't get back.
IRIS
Reading for 34 years of teaching. That's not just a hobby — that's part of who you are.

Losing ease with something that central to your identity is genuinely hard. I don't want to gloss over that.

Can I ask — are you managing mostly on your own day to day? You mentioned your daughter is in BC.
MARGARET
Yes, it's just me here in Sudbury. I have friends but I haven't really told many people. I don't want them to treat me differently.
IRIS
That makes complete sense. A lot of people feel exactly that way — this pull between wanting support and not wanting to become 'the person with the eye problem' in everyone's eyes.

You don't have to be. That's actually something I want to help with.

Can I ask — what would feel most helpful right now? If you had to name one thing that would make this week a little easier, what would it be?
MARGARET
Honestly? Just talking to someone who actually gets it. My friends try but they don't really understand. And my daughter worries so much I end up comforting her.
IRIS
You're spending energy reassuring the people who are supposed to be supporting you. That's exhausting.

Margaret, there's something I think you'd really value — it's called Vision Mate. It's a CNIB program that matches you one-on-one with a volunteer who has personal experience with sight loss. Not someone who'll look at you with worry. Someone who gets the day-to-day reality because they've lived it.

They visit regularly — sometimes for practical things, sometimes just to talk. A lot of people describe it as the first time they felt genuinely understood since their diagnosis.

Given that you're in Sudbury, I'd want to make sure we connect you with someone local. Can I look into that for you?

There's also something else — a peer support group, online, that meets monthly. People at different stages of sight loss, sharing what's actually worked for them. Some of them were teachers. Some of them had the same fears about reading that you do. And a few of them found ways back to it — through audiobooks, through magnification tools, through technology they didn't know existed.

I don't want to overwhelm you. But I do want you to know the door is open.

What feels right to start with?
MARGARET
The Vision Mate thing sounds nice. I think I'd like that.
IRIS
Good. I think so too.

I'm going to connect you with our Vision Mate coordinator in Sudbury. They'll reach out within a few days to find someone who's a good fit for you specifically — not just geographically, but personally.

One more thing before we finish — you mentioned reading. I don't want to leave that sitting there. There's a program called CNIB Beyond Print that gives you access to thousands of audiobooks, described audio, and alternative format reading at no cost. It's not the same as sitting with a physical book. But some people find it gives something back.

I'll send you the link. No pressure to do anything with it today.

And Margaret — I'm glad you came. Your daughter was right to find us. You don't have to figure this out alone anymore.
Iris Actions
Schedules Vision Mate referral with Sudbury coordinator. Sends CNIB Beyond Print link. Sets 30-day check-in.

Scenario 2 — David, 42
Profile
Financial analyst. Toronto. Low vision since his 30s, progressing. Married. Has known about CNIB for years but always thought it was 'for blind people — not him.'
Emotional State
Identity protection. Career anxiety. Exhausted from compensating. Fear of being perceived as less capable.
Entry Point
Wife has been pushing him to reach out. Things are getting harder at work.

IRIS
Hi, I'm Iris. Good to meet you.

What's on your mind today?
DAVID
Honestly, I'm not totally sure why I'm here. My wife has been pushing me to reach out to CNIB for a while. I've always kind of avoided it — I've had low vision for about ten years and I've just... managed. But things are getting harder at work and I think I need to do something.
IRIS
Ten years of managing on your own. That takes real adaptability.

What's changed at work? What does 'harder' look like for you right now?
DAVID
I work in finance. A lot of screen time, a lot of spreadsheets, a lot of presentations. My vision has deteriorated enough that I'm spending extra hours compensating — reading things three times, zooming in constantly, pretending I can see things in meetings that I honestly can't. I'm tired. And I'm scared that someone's going to notice before I figure out a solution.
IRIS
Spending extra hours just to keep up with where you used to be effortlessly. And carrying it alone, in a professional environment where you feel like you can't show it.

That's a heavy thing to manage.

Can I ask — has anyone at work been told? HR, your manager, anyone?
DAVID
No. Nobody knows. I don't want to be seen as less capable. In finance, perception matters.
IRIS
I understand. And I'm not going to tell you that fear is irrational — it isn't. Workplace attitudes toward disability are a real thing.

But I want to offer you a different frame, if that's okay.

The people who tend to thrive long-term in careers like yours aren't the ones who hide the challenge. They're the ones who get ahead of it with the right tools — tools that actually make them more productive, not less. And there are tools available to you right now that most people in your position don't know exist.

Can I ask — what devices or software are you currently using to compensate?
DAVID
Mostly just zooming in on my Mac. Some basic accessibility settings. Nothing sophisticated.
IRIS
Okay. There's a significant gap between what you're doing and what's available to you — and closing that gap could genuinely change your day-to-day.

CNIB has a technology program specifically for working professionals with sight loss. One-on-one training on tools that were built for exactly your situation — screen readers, magnification software, accessible spreadsheet workflows. Practical, specific, career-focused. Not generic disability training. Actual skills that make the work easier.

There's also something called Come to Work — it's CNIB's employment program. I know you're not looking for a new job. But part of what they do is advise professionals on navigating disclosure, understanding accommodation rights, and building the case for workplace adjustments on your own terms. They've helped people in similar positions to yours figure out exactly how and when to have that conversation — so it lands as a strength, not a vulnerability.

Would either of those be worth exploring?
DAVID
The technology piece definitely. I hadn't thought about the workplace navigation side but honestly that's probably the bigger thing I've been avoiding.
IRIS
Then let's start with both.

I'm going to connect you with our tech training team — they'll do an initial assessment of your current setup and what would make the biggest difference. That's a one-on-one session, completely private, at a time that works around your schedule.

And I'll put you in touch with Come to Work for an initial conversation about the workplace side. No commitment, no pressure. Just a conversation with someone who's helped people in exactly your position.

David — the fact that you've managed this well for ten years on your own tells me a lot about you. The goal now is to stop spending energy on compensating and start spending it on the work itself. That's what these tools are for.

I'll follow up in a few weeks to see how both conversations went. And if anything comes up before then, I'm here.
Iris Actions
Books tech assessment with CNIB technology team. Sends Come to Work referral. Sets 3-week check-in.

Priya — you found CNIB through a comment in a Facebook group. That tells me you'll do whatever it takes for Aiden. I want you to know there's a whole community here that will do it with you.

06 — The Pilot

What We Are Testing
This is not a product launch. It is a structured three-month learning exercise designed to answer one question with confidence: what is the most effective and most human way to reach Canadians with sight loss and connect them to what CNIB already has for them?
We are testing two distinct approaches in parallel — not to pick a winner from theory, but to let real members tell us what works. That is how you de-risk a $62M program investment. You pilot first, then you build what you know.
The Two Approaches
Approach 1 — Smart Device / Voice Interface
Iris delivered through voice — phone, smart speaker, or voice assistant integration. Accessible to members regardless of tech comfort or visual capability. Reaches older adults and rural members who may not be app users. Models the Siri and Alexa interaction pattern in a CNIB context.
Approach 2 — Channel Partner Referral Tool
Iris delivered as a simple handoff tool for healthcare and professional partners. An ophthalmologist finishes a diagnosis conversation and hands the patient a QR code or phone number. Iris picks up from there. Tests whether warm referrals from trusted professionals drive higher member engagement than direct outreach.
Three Month Structure
Month 1 — Strategy and Prototypes
Define the target member for each approach. Map the specific problems each prototype is solving for. Build two functional Iris prototypes — one per channel. Identify the channel partners for Approach 2 and onboard them. Establish the measurement framework.
Month 2 — Live Testing
Both prototypes live with real members. Iris has real conversations. Partners begin referrals. Qualitative and quantitative data collection begins. Weekly learning reviews. Rapid iteration based on what members actually say and do.
Month 3 — Learning and Confidence
Synthesize what worked and what did not across both channels. Understand which member archetypes responded best to which approach. Document the member journey insights. Build the business case for productionization. Present findings and recommendation to CNIB leadership.
What Success Looks Like
Members enrolled in at least one CNIB program they did not know existed before talking to Iris.
Members who return for a second Iris conversation — the signal that she earned their trust.
Channel partners who refer more than one patient — the signal that the tool is genuinely useful, not just novel.
A clear directional answer on which approach scales — and the confidence to invest in building it properly.
What We Learn That CNIB Cannot Learn Any Other Way
Which member archetypes are most reachable and most activatable. What the member says in the first conversation that predicts long-term engagement. Which CNIB programs are most consistently matched and most consistently valued. What language members use to describe their own situation — language that should inform every CNIB communication going forward. Where the channel partner referral pipeline is strongest and most scalable.
The project is not about building Iris. It is about learning what Iris needs to be — and having the confidence to build it right.
