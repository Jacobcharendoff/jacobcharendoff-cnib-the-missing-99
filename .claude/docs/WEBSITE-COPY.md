The Gap
Headline
The support exists. Most people never find it.
Body — four short paragraphs, spaced apart
Sight loss arrives fast and without a roadmap. A diagnosis. A doctor's appointment. A moment in the kitchen when something doesn't look right. And then — nothing. No one tells you what comes next.
CNIB has been building programs, services, and community for Canadians with sight loss for over a hundred years. Free programs. Real support. People who understand. And yet, of the 2.2 million Canadians living with sight loss, fewer than 13,000 are currently engaged with what CNIB offers.
That's not a funding problem. It's not a program quality problem. It's a connection problem. People who need support don't know it exists. And when they do find it, they don't know where to start.
Iris is built to solve that. Not by adding more programs. By making sure the right person finds the right one — at the right moment in their life.

Section 5 — How Iris Works
DESIGN: Light section — first time we shift from black to near-white or very dark grey. Creates visual breathing room after the demo. Three columns or a flowing vertical layout. Clean, Apple-adjacent. Icons are minimal — concentric rings at different scales for each stage. No photography.
Section label — small, yellow, all caps
The Experience
Headline
A guide who actually knows you.
Body intro
Iris isn't a directory. She isn't a chatbot. She's a guide who gets to know each member the way a good mentor would — through conversation, over time, across the real moments of their life.
Stage 1 — headline and body
She listens first.
Iris opens with the human question, not the intake form. What brought you here? What's changed? What's been the hardest part? She builds a picture of the whole person before she suggests anything.
Stage 2 — headline and body
She matches precisely.
Based on diagnosis, geography, life stage, and what matters most right now, Iris surfaces two or three programs that are actually right for this person — not a list of everything CNIB offers. She explains each one in plain language, connected to what the member just told her.
Stage 3 — headline and body
She takes the next step.
Iris doesn't end with information. She ends with action. A referral booked. A connection made. A follow-up scheduled. The member leaves having gained something real, not just a brochure.
Stage 4 — headline and body
She stays.
Iris checks in. As life changes — diagnosis progresses, circumstances shift, new needs emerge — she adjusts. The relationship deepens over time. From newly diagnosed to engaged community member. From member to mentor.
Below stages — one line
Not a one-time interaction. A lifelong guide.

Section 8 — The Pilot
DESIGN: This section is for Angela specifically — though written to be understood by anyone she shares it with. Clean, confident, direct. No charity language. No consulting language. This reads like a founder explaining what they're testing and why it matters. Timeline shown visually — three months, three phases. Minimal. Section label — small, yellow, all caps
The Pilot
Headline
Three months. Two approaches. One question.
The question — large, prominent
What is the most effective way to reach Canadians with sight loss and connect them to what CNIB has built for them?
Intro body
This is not a product launch. It's a structured learning exercise. We pilot two distinct approaches in parallel, with real members, across three months. We measure what works. We learn who responds to what. And at the end, we have the evidence and confidence to build what we know works — not what we think might.
Approach 1 — headline and body
Approach 1 — Direct member engagement.
Iris delivered through voice and conversation — phone, smart device, or voice assistant. Accessible to members regardless of technology comfort or visual capability. Designed to reach the full demographic spectrum: seniors, newly diagnosed adults, families, long-term members who don't know everything available to them.
Approach 2 — headline and body
Approach 2 — Channel partner distribution.
Iris delivered as a simple referral tool for healthcare and professional partners. An ophthalmologist finishes a difficult conversation and hands a card. A social worker makes a call. A pharmacist passes along a number. Iris picks up from there. Testing whether warm professional referrals drive stronger member engagement than direct outreach.
Timeline — three phases
Month 1 — Strategy and build.
Define the target member for each approach. Map the specific problems each prototype solves. Build both prototypes. Identify and onboard channel partners. Establish how we'll measure success.
Month 2 — Live testing.
Both approaches live with real members. Iris has real conversations. Partners begin referrals. Weekly learning reviews. Rapid iteration based on what members actually say and do.
Month 3 — Learning and confidence.
Synthesize what worked and what didn't across both channels. Understand which member types respond best to which approach. Build the evidence base for what comes next. Present findings and recommendation.
What success looks like
We're measuring three things.
Members who enroll in at least one CNIB program they didn't know existed before talking to Iris. Members who return for a second conversation — the signal that Iris earned their trust. Channel partners who refer more than one patient — the signal that the tool is genuinely useful, not just novel.
The bigger picture — one strong closing paragraph
The pilot is not the product. It's the evidence that makes the product worth building properly. Three months of real conversations with real Canadians tells us more than any amount of planning — and gives CNIB the confidence to invest in what we know works.

Copy Guidance — For the Build Team
These notes are for whoever is building the site in Cowork. They explain the intent behind specific copy choices and flag things not to change.
On the tone
Every word on this site was written to stand alone — no presenter, no context assumed. Angela may share this internally with board members, colleagues, or funders who have no prior context. The copy has to give them the full story.
The tone is confident without being arrogant, warm without being soft, specific without being jargon-heavy. If something reads like a consulting deck, it's wrong. If something reads like a charity brochure, it's wrong. It should read like a founder who believes in what they're building and has done the work to prove it.
On the stats
2.2M — CNIB's stated addressable market. 0.55% — calculated from 12,227 currently served divided by 2.2M. $62M — CNIB's annual program spend from FY2025 audited financials. These are primary-source verified. Do not round or approximate them.
On Iris's description
Iris is described as an AI member engagement guide — not a chatbot, not an assistant, not a tool. The word guide is deliberate. It carries warmth, direction, and relationship. Do not substitute synonyms.
On the pilot framing
The pilot section never uses the word 'consulting.' It never positions this as Jacob's work. It positions this as CNIB's pilot, with Iris as the thing being tested. The language throughout is 'we' — collaborative, not transactional.
