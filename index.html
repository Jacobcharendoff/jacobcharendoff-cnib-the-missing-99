The Problem

CNIB serves Canadians living with sight loss.
Most of them don't know it yet.

Roughly 1.5 million Canadians live with sight loss today. CNIB connects with about 13,000 of them. That isn't a marketing problem — it's a navigation problem. Most Canadians who need CNIB never find the door.

Meet Iris

A navigator that knows
every program, every member, every door.

Iris is an AI navigator designed for CNIB. It listens like a person, knows every program in the ecosystem, and connects each Canadian with sight loss to the support that actually fits their life — the moment they need it.

01 — Listen

Hears the whole person.

Diagnosis, life stage, fears, the things they aren't saying. No intake form. No pity. Just a conversation that meets them where they are.

02 — Understand

Knows what helps.

Every CNIB program, every eligibility rule, every regional nuance. Vision rehab, Guide Dogs, peer support, employment, technology, advocacy.

03 — Act

Makes the connection.

Books the call. Sends the warm intro. Follows up at 2am or 2pm. Brings the right CNIB human into the loop the moment one is needed.

One navigator. Every door into CNIB.

The pilot

A 90-day proof.
Built with CNIB. Measured honestly.

Iris doesn't need a five-year plan to prove itself. It needs ninety days, one region, and the willingness to measure what happens when sight loss support meets people where they are.

0
Days 1–30

Ground truth.

Embed with CNIB program leaders. Map every pathway. Train Iris on the real ecosystem — programs, eligibility, regional nuance, the things only insiders know.

0
Days 31–60

Closed pilot.

Launch with one region and a small cohort of new and existing members. Iris answers, refers, follows up. Every conversation reviewed by a CNIB human.

0
Days 61–90

Public soft launch.

Open the door wider. Medical providers, family members, educators. Measure activation, satisfaction, and the warm handoffs that actually land.

What we measure

Reach

New Canadians with sight loss who connect with CNIB for the first time.

Activation

Members who take up at least one program after talking to Iris.

Quality

Member satisfaction vs. existing intake. Conversation review by CNIB staff.

Cost per connection

All-in cost of an Iris-mediated handoff vs. traditional intake.

Provider lift

Referrals from optometrists, ophthalmologists, GPs after the medical pilot.

Reported back to CNIB leadership monthly. Public summary at day 90.

The hard parts

The questions CNIB
would ask in week one.

Iris doesn't hide from the difficult questions. Here is a plain answer to every hard thing a CNIB executive, program lead, or digital team member would raise the moment they saw a prototype like this. None of these are solved. All of them have a direction.

Bilingual — French parity, not French translation.

Canada is bilingual. CNIB is bilingual. Iris has to be built bilingual from day one, not translated at the end. The pilot launches in English first because it's faster to ship, but the 90-day plan includes a French co-build with a CNIB Quebec program lead — not a localization agency. A French-speaking member should never feel like a second priority.

Accessibility — held to a higher standard than CNIB's own site.

This is CNIB. Iris has to be the most accessible interface in the organization, not the least. That means WCAG 2.2 AA minimum, full screen reader compatibility, voice-first alternatives for every interaction, adjustable type and contrast, and regular testing with real CNIB members who use AT. The site you're looking at right now includes an accessibility panel in the top-right — and that's the floor, not the ceiling.

Privacy & PHIPA — health conversations belong to members.

A conversation with Iris will contain things that look a lot like personal health information. That means PHIPA in Ontario, equivalent provincial legislation elsewhere, and a privacy posture that assumes the member owns their data end to end. Canadian data residency. Explicit consent for storage and human review. An easy, one-step "forget me" inside the chat. CNIB's Chief Privacy Officer signs off on the architecture before pilot day one.

Data ownership — CNIB owns the member graph.

Iris is a tool, not a platform. Every conversation, every referral, every follow-up belongs to CNIB — not to a vendor, not to Jacob, not to a model provider. The data layer is portable by design. If CNIB ever wants to bring Iris in-house, the handoff is a weekend of work, not a lawsuit. This is a tool built for CNIB to own.

Cost per connection — measured against the real baseline.

Iris has to be cheaper per meaningful connection than the current intake path, and the pilot will measure that honestly. The baseline we'd propose: CNIB's current fully-loaded cost of converting an inbound inquiry into an activated program participant. Iris carries compute cost, human review cost, and an honest share of build cost. If the numbers don't work, they don't work — and the pilot is designed to tell us that in 90 days, not 90 months.

When Iris doesn't know — the human handoff is the point.

Iris is not a replacement for CNIB staff. It is a front door that makes sure the right human gets the right conversation at the right time. The Margaret demo above includes a moment where Iris explicitly says "that one I'm not the right person for" and books a peer support call. That is not a failure mode. That is the product.

What's Next

Go even further
with Iris.

The pilot is the front door. These are the rooms it opens into.

Coming soon

Dial-in phone conversations.

A real Canadian phone number. No app, no smartphone, no Wi-Fi. Pick up the receiver, dial Iris, and have the same conversation a member would have on the website. Built for the seniors and rural Canadians who are the hardest to reach — and who need CNIB the most.

Coming soon

WheelTrans & accessible transit.

Iris books the ride. Direct integration with WheelTrans in Toronto, Access Transit in Vancouver, Para Transpo in Ottawa. "Iris, I need to get to my CNIB tech assessment Tuesday at 2." Done. The single biggest reason members miss appointments is transportation. Iris solves it inside the same conversation.

Coming soon

Be My Eyes handoff.

When a member needs eyes, not a program, Iris hands the moment to Be My Eyes — the AI vision tool 19,000+ blind Canadians already use. "Iris, what's in this can?" One-tap to a sighted volunteer or GPT-4 vision. Iris stays for the navigation; Be My Eyes handles the seeing. The two best tools in the community, working as one.
