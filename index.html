The Missing 99%

A 90-day project sprint to diagnose why CNIB reaches less than 1% of more than 1.5 million Canadians with sight loss, and begin closing the gap.
The Gap

The reach problem is well documented. The causes are not.

CNIB's strategic plan, annual reports, and EY-audited financials all confirm the same picture: approximately 12,200 people served out of 1.5 million who could benefit. "The Way Forward" names this as a priority. What it does not yet contain is a detailed account of where and why people fall out of the system between first awareness and sustained engagement. That is the work this project sprint is designed to do.

Initial Hypotheses

Three places I believe the system is breaking down.

I created two programs at CNIB (the Venture Studio and the Know Your Rights podcast) and I am a member who lives with sight loss. Through that work, I watched how members move through the organization, where they get stuck, and where they quietly disappear. These hypotheses come from that firsthand exposure. They are starting points, not conclusions. The sprint is designed to validate or disprove each one.

To be clear: this is an evaluation of the system-wide member journey, not of any individual program. My direct experience is concentrated in the entrepreneurship and advocacy segments. The discovery phase will include populations I have not worked with directly, including seniors, families, children, and deafblind members.

These three hypotheses map directly to the priorities you outlined in our conversation: discovery is the acquisition layer behind the rebrand, retention is the engagement model reimagined, and scale is the AI and technology question. This sprint treats them as one connected system, not three separate initiatives.

01
Discovery
CNIB has no systematic way to reach people at the moment of diagnosis.
The 12,300 people CNIB serves today are largely self-selecting: they already identify with the sight loss community. But the vast majority of newly diagnosed Canadians never hear about CNIB from their ophthalmologist, and when they search on their own, CNIB's digital presence does not meet them where they are. There is no structured referral protocol with eye care providers, no post-diagnosis digital intake pathway, and no mechanism to even measure how many people fall through this gap. The rebrand will help, but only if there is an acquisition system behind it to capture the demand it creates.
02
Retention
People who engage with one program rarely move to another.
Vision Mate (1,488 participants) and Mentor Match (200+ pairs) work well in isolation. But there is no systematic mechanism connecting a Vision Mate participant to Guide Dogs, employment services, or the tech lending library. Each program operates as its own intake funnel. CNIB Connects, a previous digital engagement platform designed to address this, has been paused — suggesting the problem has been recognized before but not yet solved. I have seen this firsthand: in Venture Studio sessions, at Connecting the Dots events, and through conversations during Know Your Rights recordings, members repeatedly told me they had no idea other CNIB services existed. This was not one or two people. It was a pattern. I also know members who quietly stopped engaging altogether. The sprint needs to find out whether that is an isolated experience or a systemic one, and what the actual drop-off numbers look like.
03
Scale
Foundation revenue is flat while program costs grow. Serving 10x more people requires a different economic model.
CNIB's operating deficit widened from $3.2M to $7.2M in a single year, even with $6.4M in investment income offsetting the gap. Donation and fundraising revenue declined slightly to $33.4M while expenses grew 6.7% to $94M. At $5,072 per person served today, reaching even 5% of the addressable market would cost multiples of the current budget. The Foundation is already relying on investment returns to stay close to breakeven. CNIB has 3,000+ volunteers — latent capacity that represents infrastructure already in place — but activating them effectively requires the same connective tissue the member journey lacks. Technology, AI, and process redesign are not aspirational. They are the only path where the economics actually work.

Program data: CNIB Foundation Annual Report 2024–2025. Financial data: EY-Audited Non-Consolidated Financial Statements, FY2024 and FY2025.

The peer comparison above tells us something important: even the highest-performing blindness organizations globally have not cracked past single-digit reach. CNIB's <1% is low by those standards, but the ceiling that peer organizations have hit suggests closing this gap requires a fundamentally different approach to discovery and engagement, not incremental improvements to the existing model.
The Project Sprint

90 days embedded with your team to validate these hypotheses and fix what we can.

This is not an advisory engagement that produces recommendations for someone else to implement. It is a project sprint: I work inside the building with your program leads and frontline staff, with access to program data and member records, and the output is both answers and working improvements. Three phases:

Weeks 1–4
Discovery
Structured interviews with ELT, program leads, frontline staff, and community members. Mapping the member journey and documenting what has already been tried.
Weeks 5–9
Implementation
ELT sponsor decides what gets built. I implement one to two high-impact pilots based on discovery findings.
Weeks 10–12
Measurement
Formal ELT presentation: findings, pilot outcomes, and a prioritized 12-month roadmap.
Assumptions and risks: This sprint assumes access to program-level data and CRM systems, direct access to frontline staff and community members for interviews, and a designated ELT sponsor with authority to approve pilot implementations. If the data infrastructure is less mature than expected, I will flag it by week 3 with a revised timeline for ELT approval before discovery extends. If the hypotheses above prove wrong, the sprint pivots to what the evidence actually shows. If the core bottleneck turns out to be external to CNIB (e.g., physician referral behavior), the roadmap will address what CNIB can control and clearly name what it cannot. The 12-month roadmap will align to the existing pillars in "The Way Forward" (2023–2028).
Investment
$60,000
Full 90-day engagement, paid monthly ($20,000 × 3 months). Inclusive of all three deliverables, stakeholder interviews, pilot implementation, and final ELT presentation. No additional fees.

What success looks like at Day 90

Three questions answered with data that did not exist before.

Where are people dropping off?

Quantified member journey with drop-off rates at every transition point.

Does at least one pilot measurably improve a metric?

ELT sponsor and I agree on the metric and target before the pilot begins.

Can we project 12-month reach?

Evidence-based projection if roadmap recommendations are implemented. If no, the report will say so.

Why This Is Personal

I live with vision loss and I am an active CNIB member. I know the organizational culture, the program landscape, and many of the people involved because I work inside the organization today. I have also personally navigated the barriers these programs address, including filing a human rights complaint with the Ontario Human Rights Commission when the CFA Institute denied me an accommodation for my sight loss. This is not an abstract policy area for me.

Programs I Built at CNIB
Venture Studio
An entrepreneurship accelerator for CNIB members with sight loss. I designed the program structure, pitched it internally, and built it from the ground up. Originally scoped at 12 sessions, it was extended to 20 based on participant demand. The program launched October 2025 and runs through May 2026. Running it showed me the operational gaps up close: getting participants the information they needed between sessions was harder than it should have been, and there was no system to connect Venture Studio members to other CNIB services that could help them, like employment resources or the tech lending library.
Know Your Rights Podcast
A 9-episode series on the CNIB Podcast Network that I co-created and hosted. Each episode covers a different area where Canadians with sight loss face discrimination: healthcare, employment, housing, transportation, the built environment. Every episode carries Law Society of Ontario Accredited EDI Professionalism credit, and the series was covered by LawNow Magazine. After the final episode, there was no mechanism to follow up with listeners or connect them to relevant CNIB services. The content existed in isolation. That experience is part of why I believe the connective tissue between programs is the central problem.
